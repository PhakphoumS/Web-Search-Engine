          							<h1 class="title">Gentle Exercise Program Improves Outcomes among Older Home Care Clients</h1>
			          
	        
          



<article class="node-5125 node node-news node-promoted view-mode-full clearfix" about="/content/gentle-exercise-program-improves-outcomes-among-older-home-care-clients" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">

      <header>
                  <span property="dc:title" content="Gentle Exercise Program Improves Outcomes among Older Home Care Clients" class="rdf-meta element-hidden"></span>
      
          </header>
  
	<div class='news-datetime'>10/25/2019</div>
  <p>A study published in Innovation in Aging, a journal of the Gerontological Society of America, shows that a gentle exercise program delivered by home care aides improved client health, especially among participants whose caregivers are not family members.</p>
<p>Published by researchers at the University of Illinois at Chicago, the study assessed changes in clients&rsquo; physical abilities and the amount of social support provided by clients&rsquo; home care aides as a result of the exercise program.</p>
<p>&ldquo;As regular caregivers, home care aides are well-positioned to promote physical activity among frail older adults in home settings,&rdquo; said Naoko Muramatsu, the study&rsquo;s author.</p>
<p>The program, called <a href="https://www.ncoa.org/resources/program-summary-healthy-moves-for-aging-well/">Healthy Moves for Aging Well</a>, includes low-risk, gentle exercises: a seated step-in-place, arm curls and an ankle point-and-flex. During regular visits, trained home care aides administer the program, remind clients of their daily activity goals, and provide motivation to complete the exercises. Dr. Muramatsu adapted the Healthy Moves program, originally developed by Partners in Care Foundation, for use by home care aides to deliver to their clients.</p>
<p>Fifty clients, with a mean age of 76 years, and their home care aides participated in the study; 18 pairs were family members and 32 were non-family.</p>
<p>Muramatsu and her colleagues found that the Healthy Moves program was effective &mdash; clients reported being better able to complete daily tasks, like walking from room to room and pouring a drink from a carton. Objectively measured client physical performance also improved.</p>
<p>However, the clients of home care aides who were not their family members saw a greater improvement.</p>
<p>&ldquo;Healthy Moves provides training to home care aides on motivating their clients,&rdquo; said Muramatsu, professor of community health sciences in the UIC School of Public Health and fellow of the UIC Institute for Health Research and Policy. &ldquo;Family members may more naturally help their clients &mdash; who may be their parent &mdash; with physical activity, which is currently not part of home care aides&rsquo; job description.&rdquo;</p>
<p>&ldquo;Improvement in these small tasks makes a large difference when it comes to quality of life,&rdquo; Muramatsu said. &ldquo;Few physical activity programs target older adults who have difficulty with basic activities, such as standing and walking. Programs that do so effectively &mdash; through physical or occupational therapy, for example &mdash; are often too expensive for wide dissemination among this group of people. With Healthy Moves, we hope to develop a sustainable health promotion program that can be used widely by all kinds of people and organizations in community-settings.&rdquo;</p>
<p>UIC&rsquo;s Lijuan Yin is a co-author of the study, which was funded by the National Institute on Aging (<a href="/study/promoting-seniors-health-home-care-aides-pilot">R21AG042801</a> and <a href="/study/promoting-seniors-health-home-care-aides-randomized-controlled-trial">R01AG053675</a>) and the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention <a href="https://www.ihrp.uic.edu/study/iprc-workplace-health-research-network-collaborating-center">U48DP005010 SIP 14-0310</a>.</p>
<p><em>This news release was originally written for UIC News by <a href="https://today.uic.edu/staff/jacqueline-carey">Jackie Carey</a>. </em></p>
<p><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31528715">Read the article</a>:</p>
<p>Muramatsu N, Yin L. Gentle physical activity intervention led by caregivers in a Medicaid home care program: do outcomes differ between family and nonfamily caregiving dyads? Innov Aging. 2019 Sep 4;3(3):igz034.</p>
